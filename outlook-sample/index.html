2019 Outlook

U.S. Lands Softly, Converges with Rest of the World

In 2018, late-cycle fiscal stimulus in the U.S. spurred stronger growth at a time when the unemployment and savings rates were low and inflation was already slowly trending upward. U.S. economic growth will likely slow in the first half of 2019, converging with the pace of global growth as the effects of the 2017 tax reform and fiscal stimulus fade. A combination of a higher U.S. current account deficit and federal budget deficit will likely moderate the recent strong U.S. dollar trend. In emerging markets, valuations remain attractive and their long-term growth story is still positive. In the near-term, we expect a more favorable U.S.-China trade relationship and the traction of Chinese stimulus to catalyze emerging market growth, though this is not assured. In short, 2019 will likely be a story of convergence.

Asset Allocation Views

Stocks remain cheap to bonds and should continue to outperform bonds throughout the rest of this elongated cycle. Notably, U.S. corporate bonds—investment grade and high yield—remain overvalued on a relative basis and will remain so until the next recession.

International assets remain very cheap relative to the U.S. Regardless of short-term relative performance in 2019, the long-term returns of international assets are likely to be higher, given current valuations.

    The euro area economy, the second-largest in the world next to the U.S., is growing but at a slower pace than the U.S. economy. Yes, leading indicators of business activity in the European Union (EU)—as expressed by the Composite Purchasing Managers Index (PMI) for the region—remain in expansion territory. However, their current downward trajectory points to decelerating growth, and equity markets respond to change at the margin.

    Emerging market economies have faced headwinds from a stronger dollar and capital flight throughout 2018. Looking into 2019, however, we anticipate many of these trends moderating as the outperformance of the U.S. economy relative to the rest of the world fades. This should provide a supportive backdrop for most emerging market economies with sound fundamentals. China, as the most important emerging market economy, will likely face headwinds from trade disputes with the U.S. along with slowing domestic growth. We anticipate a stabilization in China as trade concerns moderate and policymakers support growth via policy easing without larger, disruptive stimulus.

Policy

  • The bellwethers that typically spell the end of an economic cycle – high and rising inflation, an inverted yield curve, and an unstable U.S. dollar – have yet to materialize. While the U.S. yield curve is slowly moving towards inversion, wage inflation has remained uncharacteristically tame. At this point in the cycle, we would expect the low unemployment rate to accelerate inflationary pressures in the form of rising wages. This force has begun to take shape over the course of 2018, but not at any scale that causes alarm.

    Source: Haver Analytics, 10/31/2018

    Indeed, with this slow-moving economic cycle, we expect the Fed’s path towards neutral and tighter monetary conditions will span years, not months. The Fed’s preferred measure of inflation, the core Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE) Index, has only just returned to a 2% growth rate, while the actual level of inflation still remains well below the pre-financial crisis trend.

  • To be fair, the European Central Bank (ECB) didn’t begin its bond-buying program until 2015, whereas the Fed began in 2008, putting Europe’s recovery several years behind the U.S. As a percentage of GDP, euro area central bank assets have almost doubled those of the U.S. Granted, the ECB remains supportive of growth with easy monetary policy. However, short-term interest rates are already at rock-bottom levels, and the Governing Council has been tapering its asset purchases, with a plan to end the program in January 2019.

    Source: Haver Analytics, 10/31/2018

  • The trajectory for most emerging market policy rates remains in a downward and supportive path thanks to slower inflation and improved fiscal positions. There are exceptions, however, including Turkey and Argentina that will likely need to defend their currencies and shore up their current account deficits in 2019 through more aggressive policy tightening. On balance, however, we expect most emerging market policymakers will be dovish as the fundamentals of most economies remain sound. We foresee Chinese policymakers remaining supportive through targeted growth-stabilizing stimulus. China’s goal in 2019 will likely be to shore up faltering export-dependent businesses in the face of trade conflict with the United States.

    Source: The People’s Bank of China, Bloomberg, 9/30/18 *Light gray shading indicates periods of Chinese monetary policy easing.

Valuation

  • The outlook for equities broadly remains positive, despite stretched valuations in the United States and higher market volatility. Opportunities remain in U.S. equities, but selectivity remains key. International and emerging market stocks present more compelling value at this point in the cycle, but a mixed outlook suggests a catalyst is necessary to bring investor flows. Within fixed income, credit spreads remain tight by historical standards in the United States, and the outlook for bonds suggests investors should not expect anything more than coupon returns over the course of 2019.

  • The MSCI Europe Index’s price-to-sales (P/S) ratio has become extremely cheap relative to that of the MSCI ACWI and USA Index. While investors can pay only $1.24 for every $1.00 of European sales, the deepest discount to the U.S. since the early 2000s, the price could get even cheaper without a catalyst. It’s worth noting that the last time Europe traded at such a discount (i.e., 2003), it enjoyed its longest period of outperformance since the 1970s. While compelling valuation opportunities exist in Europe at a time when the ECB is lending a helping hand, slowing growth means that it could take more time to unlock the potential reward embedded in share prices.

  • Valuations remain compelling in both emerging market equities and fixed income. Across the investable universe, emerging market assets remain some of the cheapest available to investors today based on relative valuations. This may provide an attractive entry point for investors seeking income in emerging market fixed income or capital appreciation in emerging market equities. With the headwind of a stronger U.S. dollar likely abating, emerging market assets remain among our favorite opportunities in 2019.
